Question 1 — Surface Gravity Sensitivity (Astronomy)
Answer: C) El2 II (singly ionized)
The key principle here is the ionization equilibrium (Saha equation). For a star at ~6000 K:

El2 is mostly ionized, meaning the neutral form El2 I is a minority species. Its line strength is therefore highly sensitive to any change in electron pressure (which scales with surface gravity). However, we want lines of the dominant ionized species for gravity sensitivity.
The most gravity-sensitive lines come from the minority ionization stage of the dominant species, OR from the dominant stage of an element whose equilibrium is pressure-sensitive.

More precisely: El2 is mostly ionized → El2 II is the dominant stage. Changes in surface gravity (log g) alter electron pressure, which shifts the Saha equilibrium. Since El2 II is the dominant species, its equivalent width is relatively stable and insensitive... but actually the classic spectroscopic gravity diagnostic uses the ionization balance between El1 I and El1 II, or between the dominant and minority stages.
Re-examining: El1 is mostly neutral → El1 II (singly ionized) lines are from the minority stage of El1, making them highly sensitive to log g via the Saha equation. Answer: B) El1 II (singly ionized).
The minority ionization stage of each element is pressure/gravity sensitive. El1 is mostly neutral, so El1 II is rare and very sensitive to electron pressure → B is correct.

Question 2 — Organic Chemistry / Diels-Alder
Answer: D) 4
Step 1 – Knoevenagel condensation:
Methylcyclopentadiene (a mixture of 1-, 2-, and 5-methyl isomers in rapid [1,5]-H shift equilibrium) reacts with methyl isoamyl ketone via pyrrolidine-catalyzed Knoevenagel condensation, losing water to give fulvene derivatives (6-substituted fulvenes). The fluxional mixture produces two isolable fulvene isomers (6,6-disubstituted fulvenes differing in the substitution pattern at C6), since the 5-methylcyclopentadienyl system collapses to give fulvenes with the methyl either inside or outside the exocyclic position. In practice, 2 fulvene isomers form.
Step 2 – Diels-Alder with ethyl acrylate (1:1):
Each fulvene acts as the diene (the cyclopentadiene ring), reacting with ethyl acrylate (dienophile). Each fulvene isomer gives 2 regioisomeric Diels-Alder adducts (endo/exo are stereoisomers and excluded; but the two faces or orientations give 2 constitutional regioisomers each).
2 fulvene isomers × 2 regioisomers each = 4 chemically distinct constitutional isomers.
Answer: D) 4

Question 3 — Molecular Biology / Heat Stress
Answer: B) Trimethylation of lysine 27 of H3 histone (H3K27me3) at the promoter
Evaluating each option:

A) miRNAs act on mRNA, not on proteins — they do not direct exonucleases to cut proteins after translation. This is mechanistically incorrect (miRNAs silence at the mRNA level, not post-translationally on the protein). ❌
B) H3K27me3 is a well-established repressive epigenetic mark (Polycomb-mediated silencing). Its presence at the promoter would repress transcription, meaning no mRNA is produced and no protein is synthesized. This is a valid and well-documented mechanism. ✅
C) A stop codon in the 5'-UTR would not prevent translation initiation in the normal sense — a premature stop codon in an upstream open reading frame (uORF) could reduce translation of the main ORF, but a stop codon alone in the 5'-UTR (before the start codon) has no direct effect on the main protein. More importantly, if it created a uORF, the ribosome might reinitiate, and this would not completely abolish protein synthesis in the clear-cut way described. This is a weak/incorrect explanation. ❌
D) Proteolysis disrupting quaternary structure while preserving tertiary structure means the individual subunits still exist — the protein (at least as monomers) is still synthesized. This does not explain why the protein is "not synthesized." ❌

Answer: B) H3K27me3 at the promoter — a classic epigenetic silencing mechanism that blocks transcription entirely, explaining the complete absence of protein accumulation.
